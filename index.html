Research overview

Evaluation of Explainable AI for Time Series

My PhD research focuses on evaluating explanations of AI decisions, primarily in the context of time series. This page collects various projects that I have worked on in my PhD, including computational evaluation of explainble AI (XAI) explanations, and more human-centric evaluation.

Perturbation metrics

Class-dependent evaluation effects

Perturbation analysis is often used to evaluate whether feature attributions are correct. In the below projects, we studied when that evaluation depends on the predicted class, and what that means for trusting the perturbation metrics.

Paper 1

Class-dependent perturbation effects in evaluating time-series attributions

Takeaway: perturbation-based metrics depend very much on their parameters, and can look strong for one class and weak for another, even within the same dataset and model, without us knowing why that happens.

We evaluate time-series attribution methods across datasets, model architectures, and perturbation strategies. The results suggest that class-aware reporting may be needed, because aggregate scores can hide systematic differences between classes.

Baer, G., Grau, I., Zhang, C., & Van Gorp, P. (2025). Class-Dependent Perturbation Effects in Evaluating Time Series Attributions. In R. Guidotti, U. Schmid, & L. Longo (Eds.), Explainable Artificial Intelligence (pp. 292–314). Springer Nature Switzerland. https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-032-08330-2_14

Paper 2

Why do class-dependent evaluation effects occur?

Takeaway: class-dependent effects can arise from simple differences in synthetic features, and perturbation scores can disagree with ground-truth metrics.

We use controlled synthetic time series where the relevant feature locations are known. This lets us compare perturbation-based degradation scores with ground-truth evaluation and study when the two views of attribution correctness diverge.

Baer, G., Grau, I., Zhang, C., & Van Gorp, P. (2026). Why do Class-Dependent Evaluation Effects Occur with Time Series Feature Attributions? A Synthetic Data Investigation. In I. Koprinska, J. Mendes-Moreira, & P. Branco (Eds.), Machine Learning and Principles and Practice of Knowledge Discovery in Databases (pp. 380–395). Springer Nature Switzerland. https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-032-19105-2_27

DOI Open access

Human outcomes

Metrics and human understanding

A computational score is useful only if we know what it tells us. This project studies how explanation correctness relates to whether people can understand AI decision patterns.

User study

Does explanation correctness matter?

Takeaway: lower correctness can reduce human understanding, but it may not be a one-to-one mapping, and there strong individual differences.

Participants used explanations to predict an AI system's decisions in a time-series task where we manipulated the correctness of the explanations. Correctness of explanations affected participants understanding as measured by their prediction accuracy, but not at every level.

Baer, G., Zhang, C., Grau, I., & Van Gorp, P. (2026). Does Explanation Correctness Matter? Linking Computational XAI Evaluation to Human Understanding. https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.25251v1

DOI arXiv

Synthetic data

Tools for controlled evaluation

xaitimesynth is a small package for synthetic time-series XAI evaluation. It creates datasets with known ground truth so attribution methods can be tested in controlled settings.

Package

xaitimesynth: A Python Package for Evaluating Attribution Methods

Takeaway: The package offers a lot of control for more realistic synthetic time series and the evaluation of XAI explanations on that data.

The package provides dataset generation tools, examples, and documentation for evaluating feature attributions on synthetic time series. It can be useful when you want a controlled setting before moving to messier real-world data.
